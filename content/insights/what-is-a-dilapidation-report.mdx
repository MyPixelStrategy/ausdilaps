A dilapidation report — also called a property condition report — is a photographic and written record of the condition of a construction site's adjoining structures and surrounding areas. It's undertaken before and after works to identify any changes. When a damage claim is made, it's the evidence that defends the project.

## What's included in a dilapidation report

A complete report documents the property inside and out:

- **A detailed description** of each inspected structure — its age, construction type and notable features.
- **Existing damage and defects** — cracks, settling, movement, leaks and wear — recorded with severity and location.
- **A photographic and video record** — high-resolution, location-referenced imagery that is defensible, not just a folder of photos.
- **Geo-referenced imagery** — GPS-logged capture, so pre- and post-works images compare to the exact same spot.
- **Recommendations** where existing issues are identified, and a clear **summary of findings** signed off by an engineer.

## When do you need one?

Reports are typically required before construction, renovation or demolition on neighbouring properties — and are often a condition of a development application (DA) consent. You'll usually need one when:

- A DA consent condition requires condition reports on adjoining properties.
- Vibration-sensitive works are beginning near third-party property.
- There's neighbour-complaint risk or an insurance / legal exposure clause.
- Major nearby works are underway — roadworks, excavation, tunnelling or high-rise development.

Owners come at this from a different angle to builders. For the owner's side — which reports to commission over a property's life, and when — this [owner's guide to commissioning reports](https://www.sigmasync.com.au/thinking/reports-a-real-owner-commissions) covers the ground.

## Why the pre- and post-construction pair matters

The value is in the pair. The **pre-construction report** establishes the documented condition of every adjoining property before works begin — the reference point against which any later claim is measured. The **post-construction report** is a matching record after works complete, identifying any change so that damage is proven or disproven on the evidence, not on argument.
